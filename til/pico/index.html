Raspberry Pi Pico

“If parents want to give their children a gift, the best thing they can do is to teach their children to love challenges, be intrigued by mistakes, enjoy effort, and keep on learning.” — Carol Dweck

About the size of a stick of gum, the Raspberry Pi Pico is what's called a microcontroller development board. I'm using it alongside a Pi 5 single-board computer to experiment with physical computing projects (like a burglar alarm) that need components like lights, buttons, and sensors.

The Pi 5 has 40 built-in pins you can use similarly for prototyping. However, it's not ideal as the pins are packed really close together and it already has your monitor, power cable, and anything else connected. That’s why I’ve found it’s absolutely worth getting a Pico. It’s inexpensive, and its pins are nicely spread out, so you can build the entire physical setup right on the breadboard. This makes it much easier to see exactly what plugs in where.

I'm using the Pico 2 W which adds built-in Wi-Fi and Bluetooth. See the Pi Pico 2 W Pinout for pinout diagram.
